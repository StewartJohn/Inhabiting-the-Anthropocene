Themes that Emerged in Our Posts

The members of our team shared a very general understanding of habitability as a recursive process, by which a landscape is made habitable by the efforts of its inhabitants, who transform it in order to attain that goal—but where those transformations may in fact compromise its habitability; that is the manifest challenge of the Anthropocene.

However, the authors brought more particularized interests to the project as well, leading them to raise other issues and explore other questions. Once all the posts were written the authors met to reflect on the series as a whole, and to discuss additional concerns that had emerged. The themes listed below seemed most significant. Not all of them came up in every post posts—but each came up frequently enough that we can speak of posts clustering around several emergent themes. Some authors held similar views on some themes; there were also revealing divergences. The following list indicates some of the structure of the conceptual terrain the authors explored with their posts, so to speak. The posts can be interpreted as sights seen on pathways flagged by the questions that articulate each theme.

Following each description are brief explanations we used to sort posts on that theme into two broad groups. Posts in a group are by no means in complete agreement--but they share a similar kind of emphasis. We do not mean to suggest a strict categorization with these labels. Rather, their ultimate function is to help us articulate ways that two posts can bee seen as similar or different. For convenience we label the two views (indicated by italics)--though the labels are meant to be suggestive, not precise.

Limits. The recognition that human beings are profoundly adept at altering their environment to make it more habitable reinforces a conception of ourselves that we are capable of transcending constraints to our way of life apparently imposed on us by nature. Does humanity indeed have the potential to escape natural limits? Or, are our modes of habitation subject to the kinds of limitations expressed by the proposal that there are “planetary boundaries”—parameters which determine the conditions under which the Earth system is habitable? Alternatively, whether or not technology makes habitation feasible outside those boundaries, are there moral limits to human habitation practices which demand consideration of whether all people—and other species—have fair access to habitability?
Labels for alternative views: Emphasis on limitations on habitation set by physical factors (e.g. planetary boundaries)/ emphasis on limitations on habitation set by moral factors (e.g. justice).
